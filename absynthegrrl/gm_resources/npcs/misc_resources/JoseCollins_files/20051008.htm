Anna Held leaves Hammerstein's
Lyric Theatre for Koster & Bial's,
New York, November 1897;
and a word about free publicity

Anna Held (1872-1918), Polish born singer and theatrical celebrity

(photo: Aimé Dupont, New York, circa 1897)

'Anna Held Out of the Cast.
'Her Manager Tells of a Misunderstanding with Mr. Hammerstein.
'Miss Anna Held is no longer in the cast of La Poupee at Hammerstein's Lyric Theatre, and her manager, F. Ziegfeld, says it is because of a misunderstanding between himself and Mr. Hammerstein.
'The theatre proprietor says Miss Held was physically unable to do the rôle justice and therefore ordered the understudy [Louise Hoppner] to be ready to go on for the performance. Mr. Hammerstein says he is under no financial obligations to Mr. Ziegfeld.
'Francis Wilson telegraphed Mr. Hammerstein from Syracuse, making him an offer for the opera.'
(The New York Times, New York, Monday, 1 November 1897, p.16d)

'Koster & Bial's.
'Tonight ANNA HELD. Marie Lloyd. Faust Ballet. Alice Atherton and foreign stars. Adm. 50c. Matinee Saturday.'
(The New York Times, New York, Tuesday, 9 November 1897, p.12g, advertisement)

'The number of actors and actresses who spin along the bridle paths in Central Park every morning offers undreamed of possibilities to the keen-eyed press agent, and it is strange that up to date this fact has not been taken advantage of. Maxine Elliott and her sister Gertrude are among the number of equestriennes who never fail to take a canter when the morning is fine, and Anna Held, whose milk baths and similar press agent stories won columns of free reading notices not so long since, is another.
'Now that the milk-bath story has died out, an exciting runaway would prove a sensational story, or a double hit could be made if Nat Goodwin, who rides every day, could play the rôle of the daring rescuer. Marie Lloyd is another of the footlight celebrities who finds equestrianism a delightful form of exercise.'
(The New York Times, New York, Sunday, 14 November 1897, p.20c)

'Anna Held introduced a novelty in her act last night at Koster & Bial's in the shape of a man who impersonates a horse, to whom she sings her song, "Come Play With Me".'
(The New York Times, New York, Thursday, 18 November 1897, p.7c)

Belle Blanche at the
Grand Opera House, Pittsburgh,
week beginning Monday, 31 January 1910

Belle Blanche (fl. early 20th Century)
American vaudeville entertainer, 'The Incomparable Mimic'

(photo: unknown, USA, circa 1908)

'Speaking of mimicry, there is Belle Blanche, for example, who imitates players grave and gay with entire credit to the performers. Miss Blanche's talent, while not entirely mimetic, has been largely developed in the line of impersonation and although nature has endowed her with voice and personality sufficient to win success in the legitimate field or in musical comedy, the broadening of her gifts as a mimetic and her success have tended to keep her in this line. She has a most extensive repertoire from which she may cull without premeditation the lights and shadows that reflect other great artists.>br> 'She is so versatile that her impersonations run the gamut of all the emotions and in quick succession she shows us flashes from the serious and the frivolous in dramatic art, from the highly emotional roles of Bernhardt to the entirely humorous and somewhat frivolous ditties of Harry Lauder, with the gradations of histrionic art well marked between her study of David Warfield in The Music Master has been accounted the best impersonation of that serious actor that the American stage has had. So, too, her mimicry of the Lloyd sisters, Alice and Marie, the English comediennes; of Eddie Foy, the American buffoon; of Mrs. Pat Campbell, the English emotional actress; of Ethel Barrymore, Maud Adams and a score of others. Miss Blanche's voice possesses the quality of flexibility in a rare degree and she has a mobility of countenance that adapts itself readily to each of her characterizations. Whether you see her as a musical hall [sic] favorite or a queen of the tragic drama, you see her at her best.'
(The Pittsburgh Post, Pittsburgh, Sunday, 30 January 1910, p.8d/e)

The London Our Miss Gibbs company,
including Fred Wright, Mollie Lowell,
Gordon Kent, Jean Aylwin and Julia James,
arrives in New York, August 1910

Julia James (1890-1964), English actress and singer

(photo: Rita Martin, London, 1908/09)

'Crossing [the Atlantic from London] with the Our Miss Gibbs company was a liberal education in how to acquire cheerfulness under homeskickness. Fred Wright, the comedian, displayed a photograph of curly haired, three-year-old Fred Wright, Jr., who hugged the paternal leg and begged "papa not to go on the ship and leave me."
'When the memory became too poignant Mr. Wright seized some member of the English company, pulling him or her bodily out of the deck chair, and marching the thirteen laps around the ship's deck that made a mile.
'Beautiful Molly Lowell, pulling the corner of her steamer hood over her eyes when the image of the daughter with whom she had parted at Southampton was too insistent, started the American song, "John Brown."
'Gordon Kent, like Miss Lowell (his "first time over"), kept down the lump in his throat by practising love songs in his sweet, lyric tenor in the saloon every afternoon at three. Ethel Cadman, coming back for her second season in The Arcadians after a nine days' visit to her English home, assured them: "American isn't so bad, you know. You may quite like it. I do." Clever Jean Aylwin retold every Scotch joke she knew to prop up her sinking spirits.
'Little Julia James, the youngest cone of the company, who came from school at Margate to the stage of London three years ago, following Denise Orme when that stage beauty forsook the musical comedy stage for matrimony, and who is to sing "Bedtime at the Zoo," cheered herself and combated seasickness with Scandinavian philosophy.'
(The New York Dramatic Mirror, New York, Saturday, 20 August 1910, p.4a)

José Collins to star
in the title role of Suzi,
an operetta produced by Lew M. Fields,
Casino Theatre, New York, 3 November 1914

José Collins (1887-1958), English star of musical comedy

(photo: Gould & Marsden, New York, 1914)

'Suzi Cast Engaged.
'In addition to Jose Collins, whom Lew Fields has engaged for the title-role in Suzi, the new operetta from the Hungarian, others in the cast will be Connie Ediss, Robert Evitt [i.e. Robert Evett], an English tenor; Lew Hearn, who has been at the London Hippodrome for two years; Fritzi von Busing, Melville Stewart, Alexander Clark, Arthur Lipson, Howard Benham, Betty Lee, and Doris La France, a dancer from the Winter Garden, Berlin. There will be a chorus of about forty. After a short out of town engagement the play will be seen here [in New York] about Nov. 1.'
(The New York Dramatic Mirror, New York, Saturday, 7 October 1914, p.11a)

'Miss Collins as Star.
'Mme. [Emma] Trentini, the noted grand opera singer, will give her most ambitious operetta in The Peasant Girl in Philadelphia next week and Jose Collins, one of the most remarkable of the light opera artists, will make her initial appearance in that city as a star at the same time. Mme. Trentini will open her engagement at the Lyric Christmas Eve. Miss Collins will begin a limited engagement at the Adelphi in the new light opera, Suzi, Monday evening [21 December 1914]. A coincidence in connection with the appearances of these stars, is that each will be supported by famous characters. Clifton Crawford, of Three Twin fame, will supply the principal comedy foil for Trentini, while Tom McNaughton, the English comedian, will cavort before the footlights for comedy reasons with Miss Collins.'
(Trenton Evening Times, Trenton, New Jersey, Saturday, 19 December 1914, p.8d)

ATSF 3768

          Locomotive 3768 is one of the 4-8-4 or Northern type wheel arrangement, constructed by Baldwin Locomotive Works of Philadelphia, PA, and delivered to the Santa Fe in May 1938 at a cost of $178,767.17

       No. 3768, as were other locomotives of the 3765 class, was initially assigned to passenger train service between La Junta, Colorado and Los Angeles, California, pulling trains such as the "Chief", "California Limited", "Fast Mail Express" and "Navajo".

       These locomotives could develop 5450 cylinder horsepower at 60 mph and 4450 drawbar horsepower at 50 mph.  The could be safely operated at 90 mph.  In contrast, the modern diesel-electric locomotive of today averages nearly 3000 horsepower. Amtrak’s "Southwest Chief" operates over the same route as the "Chief" with 15 cars and requires two diesel-electric locomotives for a total of 8000 horsepower to do the same thing 3768 did at 90 mph.

       With the onset of the dieselization of the Santa Fe’s transcontinental passenger trains, the 3765 class engines were transferred to freight service.  Locomotive 3768's original boiler, made of nickel steel, was replaced in December 1951. After this work, the engine saw only one more full year of service.  It was taken out of service at Argentine (Kansas City, Kansas) in August 1953 and stored serviceable.  3768 was the only one of eleven of the class 3765 to survive the scrapper's torch.  

       In May 1958, No. 3768 was presented to the City of Wichita and initially displayed at Friends’ University.  It moved from this site to Union Station in April 1977, remaining on display until being moved to Santa Fe’s North Yards (Wichita) to be turned from facing north to facing south.  While it was at North Yards, members of the GPTM took preliminary steps to a cosmetic restoration of this locomotive.  The date of arrival of the locomotive at the museum site was January 1, 1985.

       No. 3768 was donated to the Great Plains Transportation Museum, Inc. by Multimedia CableVision, the present owners of the former Wichita Union Station. To fully restore 3768 we need plus $1 million.

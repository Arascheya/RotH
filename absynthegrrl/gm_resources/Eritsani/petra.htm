Petra

RICHARD AND SAUL'S EXCELLENT ADVENTURE IN PETRA - June 1995

My friend Saul and I had been discussing a trip to the ancient city of Petra ever since we arrived in Eilat on our 3 month work term. We had heard stories from friends about the beauty and grandeur of the place and were determined to visit.

As our work term came to an end we realized how we had been putting off the trip. We knew that if we didn't do it the last weekend we were in Eilat, we would never go. So Thursday afternoon, after packing and cleaning the apartment the whole morning we set off with our bags and cameras ready for an adventure. We had bought some food and water earlier that day for the days we would be in the park exploring Petra. Saul had to go to the bank before catching the bus to get money for the trip. We knew how much the border taxes were, but didn't know how much we would spend for a hotel and meals.

When we got to the instant teller machine we discovered that here was a power failure. It wasn't just at the bank, or only in Eilat, but across the entire country. This was not a good sign at the start of a trip. We waited awhile at the bank machine hoping the power would return. We must have told 30 people that there the machines didn't work because there was no power. Not wanting to wait any longer we went to the bus station to see if the bus was still running to the border. After confirming this Saul went home to get some American money he had kept for his trip to Europe.

We were set on going that day and nothing was going stop us from crossing the border before it closed. Just as Saul returned the power was restored to our area (the rest of Israel took a bit longer). This was a sure sign that our trip must continue.

We got to the border and 30 minutes later we were standing in Jordan. We had heard horror stories from people of spending several hour waiting to get into Jordan. But there were only a few people crossing in our direction. Several tour groups passed through the other way, and I am sure they took a bit longer. On the Israeli side we got our passports stamped and Saul changed some Shekels (Israeli money) into Dinars. I had heard that it would be cheaper to change Dollars into dinars on the other side. This proved true. It costs 47 shekels ($23Can at the time) to leave Israel and 31 Dinar ($65Can) for Canadians to enter Jordan. This is the highest entrance fee of any country. why do we pay more? Most likely because we are so nice don't complain about it. On the Jordanian side we got our entrance visas and I exchanged money.

Surprisingly they didn't check our bags. They had an x-ray machine, but no one indicated for us to go over to it. We walked pass the check post and were on our way.

The bus system in Jordan is not very good. It is better to take taxis between the major cities. We waited at the border for about 20 minutes until the first taxi arrived. We had met three other travelers going together to Petra and decided it would be cheapest to share a taxi. The taxi driver didn't speak a word of English. Not surprising in a Country that for years wasn't friendly with the West. Unfortunately the taxi driver would only take up to a maximum of 4 people. The cost was a standard fare of 25 Dinar ($55Can) and Saul and I were not going to pay it so we let the others have the taxi. This turned out to be the best thing we could have done. We learned later that that taxi had broken down in the middle of the desert on the way to Petra. The people had to wait for another taxi to arrive and take them the rest of the way.

We waited another 20 minutes in the hot sun (40C in the shade) and decided to take the next taxi that came by since it was getting last. The driver was nice and only charged us 20 Dinar since there were only two of us. It was also his last fare and he was son his way home. He spoke English well. He had a few years of English in grade school about 20 years ago. With the tourism boom in Jordan he has had a lot of practice with his English. He bought us Cokes along the way and even stopped to give an old Bedouin man some water. Jordan looks very much like Israel. The south is full of mountains and is very hot. The further north we went into the hills the cooler it got.

From the people we met at the border we learned you could sleep on the roof on a hotel called the "Sunset" for 3 Dinar. It sounded perfect to us, but when we got there they said you couldn't sleep on the roof and the only room they had was 30 Dinar for a double with a bath. This was out of our price range. We had expected to spend no more than 20 Dinar for the three nights. We left and met another guy (American) just arriving who had had the same idea of sleeping on the roof. After setting him straight on it we waited for some friends of his and when they arrived we all started walking up in to Wadi Musa (the Jordanian city above Petra). We were joined by another group of three just arriving for the night and the eight of us went in search of a place to sleep.

A man approached us and asked us if we had anywhere to stay. After hearing of our problem he immediately offered us the roof of his house for 2 Dinar a night. This was fine with us because at that point we were exhausted and just wanted somewhere to crash. We drove out of the city and through the country side until we came to the house. It was in a 'suburb' of Wadi Musa. We overlooked a valley through which one of streams that would run through the park. It turned out that he runs a bed and breakfast with his four brothers.

The house was very nice with several rooms upstairs and a large room on the main floor. Once we got there he told us we could sleep inside. We were ready to sleep on the roof and bargained with him. In the end we got to sleep in the house on mattress for the same price as the roof. He also provided dinner (3-4 Dinar), breakfast (1.5 Dinar) and a ride to and from the Park. This was like a dream come true for Saul and I. It worked fine until the last day when they started to add extra charges onto our bill. Luckily it didn't spoil our stay too much.

You meet interesting people on trips. It isn't like going somehwere with your friends and not socializing with others. In a foreign country you are almost forced to socialize with others who speak your language. At the hotel we stayed at we were with some Americans about our age and a South African in his fourties, and a couple of Germans similar in age to us. Some of use we travelling just for a few days and had big bags. Some of the other had been travelling for a few months and had smaller bags than us. In the park we met some interesting people. There was a couple from the Netherlands who were in their seventies. They had just been through Syria and the rest of Jordan. They planned on seeing all of Petra in one day. We saw them all over the park and as the day went on their pace didn't slow a bit. I hope to be that active in my later years.

The next day we were up bright and early and at the park by 8am. We each bought a guide book and with three of our new friends from the night before we set off into the park. When we were at Petra there were three types of tickets. The one day pass cost 20 dinar and the two day pass 25 dinar. There was also a 3 day pass for 30 dinar, but I didn't see anyone buy it. You are required to sign your name on the pass and not give it to another person, but if you are on a budget and 20 dinar is too much for one day's touring, you can always find a new arrival willing to split the cost of a two day pass with you.

Immediately upon entering you get confronted by bedouins offering you a ride to the ancient city. you can ride on a donkey, or a horse or in a cart. You have to negotiate before the ride how much and where the ride will take you. You don't really need a horse going in but may want a ride out of the park at the end of the day. On your walk into the part you immediately see several tombs along the way into the city. these two are known as the Obelisk Tomb and, below it, the Bab El --Siq Triclinium (Door of the Gorge Triclinium). These appear impressive, but pale in comparison to the real treasures awaiting you. You come to a dam that was built in 1963 after 28 tourists were killed by a flash flood, and start walking down the Sib (a dry river bed). As you enter the siq you see two arches on the cliff walls. They are the remains of an aquaduct that brought water to the ancient city. Along both sides of the siq are tall mountain slopes that reach 200 to 300 feet in the air. Along the walls of the siq at eye level you can recognize water channels. They were chiseled out of the rock and allowed water to flow down the siq to the city.

You walk for about 20 minutes until you reach the first and most impressive building in all of Petra, El Kazneh. This building rises 120 feet in front of you. A two level building that makes you feel like an ant walking into someone's home. It was thought to be sculpted in the first century B.C. there are several carvings of human forms on its face. there are six giant pillars carved out of the first level that actually support nothing. the second level is symetrical, but smaller.

Its origin can be identified by its name The treasury of the Pharaoh Picture. As well as a treasury (select here to view the treasury) it is thought to have been a temple to Isis because of of the typical Isis symbol carved on its the face. The beauty is only surface deep because inside is an undecorated large room with two smaller rooms adjoined to it. All the tombs in Petra are like this. On the outside, built on a grand scale, but inside very plain. El Kazneh was used in Indiana Jones and the Last Crusade. It is the best preserved building because it was protected from the elements by the high mountains that surround it.

Further into the city you see other tombs, not so grand but still impressive. There are tombs with names like the Urn Tomb, the Silk Tomb and the Palace Tomb. There are many other things to see in the park as well. You can walk up 900 steps and reach one of the high places of sacrifice used by the Nabatians. As you walk around the ceremonial area you can make out the different areas. The place where the animals were prepared and the altar of sacrifice. You can also see the channels where the blood of the sacrifices would run down into collecting jars. The high place of sacrifice overlooks the entire ancient city and reveals to you many of the tombs carved into the sides of the mountains. Also on this height are two obelixes, each 20 feet tall. To carve the obelixes, the Nabatians cut away the entire mountain top around them until they were at their desired height. There are many tombs along the way down from the high place as well as water channels. The Nabatians were expert at collecting the water that fell in the hills around the city. While there was little rain during the hot summer months, there was plenty of water for public fountains and crops to support the large city's population.

Another walk is up to the Monastery (Ed Deir), a building on the scale of the treasury. It is carved 150 feet wide and 130 feet tall.(select here to view the monastery). The facade has two geometrically balanced levels. Inside on the far end is a large niche carved for the revered god of the place. There are small crosses etched in the wall by Christians to purify it from its pagan origins.

You can climb up to the top of this building and see how

high up you are, about 1000 feet above the valley floor (you can even

look through the mountains and see Israel). At the top of the building

is a large urn carved out of the rock. Standing next to it you feel

like a child looking up at a high table. It is called the

monastery because it was consecrated by Christian monks during the

12th century C.E.

There is also the ruins of a Roman city, built after The

Nabatians were defeated by Rome. There is an amphitheatre that was carved

out of the rock. It has a capacity of 3000 spectators and rivals in size

any other I have seen in Israel or Italy. What makes it impressive is

the fact that it was cut out of the rock. In the entire park there is

only one masonry building. The Qaser el bint was used as a Temple when

it was contructed in the first century BCE. It is a truly impressive

building. not only because it is standing after all these years, but the

decorations and stone arch that spands the large entrance way. In front

are the remains the large altar platform. Standing on the platform you

get just a sense of what it must have been like when the temple was in

use. This is what Saul and I saw the first day we were there.

If you go on an organized tour you will only spend about 5

hours in the park. You will see the Treasury, maybe the high place, the

treasury, and if you hurry the monastery. We spent 10 hours in the park

that first day. On the second day Saul and I went on our own. We saw

several other tombs and then decided to be adventurous. We found

some steps carved into the side of a cliff away from the main

tourist area. We made our way up to the top. At some times we had

carved stairs to follow. At other times we had to pull ourselves up

by way of small foot and hand hold (at 400 feet above the valley

floor). We found ourselves at the top overlooking the entire ruins of

Petra. We could see people across the valley on the other high place

(lower than where we were, of course) and all of the tombs we had visited

the day before. Walking around the plateau, we found several more high

places of sacrifice. We were completely alone up there, not another

tourists or bedouins. It was enchanting. We sat up there looking out at

the scenery. Saul even wrote a postcard to his parents. I left my bag

for about 10 minutes in complete confidence that it would be there when I

got back.

 

Continuing on our adventurous way, we went further along the

plateau and saw several more ruins. One was a small hut, probably

built by Bedouins. Another was a large water reservior build by the

Namibians to collect rain water. Continuing on we found ourselves on

the top of the cliff directly opposite from the treasury. We had been

impressed to see the building from the ground. But from above, it was

magnificent. We could see so much more detail. We took several pictures

and from below heard people calling up to us. Some of the Bedouins selling

souvenirs were motioning for us to move a way from the edge. We

speculated that they were afraid we would knock some rocks down and hit

some of the tourists. We walked around a bit more and then had lunch

half way up the mountain. We were completely alone except for two

Bedouin girls singing on another mountain top.

When we got back down to the valley floor we continued to walk around

some of the tombs (select here to view a tomb) further out from the main

city ruins. The Bedouins use these as stables for their donkeys. They

look nice from the outside but stink inside. We next walked into a Wadi

that took us right into the ancient city.

We next hiked up to a crusader fortress that overlooks the

ancient city from the west side. Compared to the rest of the park

it was disappointing. We climbed up and up and up. At the top

you can see the outline of the fortress, but no walls remain

standing. On the way down we passed several interesting rooms. one had

many niches carved into the walls. The other was an infinished tomb.

The Nabatians built their tommbs by cutting out the rock at the top of a

tomb and removing the rock until they reached the floor level.

By this time we had been in the part for 7 hours that day. We were

tired and ready to go home. But, the day before we had heard that there

was a small pool of water in one of the wadis. Saul and I knew that this

would be the be topper to our trip. Relaxing in a cool pool of fresh

water after hiking in the desert all day. We had to walk about 20

minutes along the wadi to get to it. We had no clue how far it was, but

we caught sight of the stream and followed it to a small pool. It may

not have been "The Pool" but it was good enough for us. The water was

cool and felt so good. While we were there a Bedouin came by and sat on

a rock near the pool. He didn't get in the water, but sat and watched

us. After 10 minutes we got out and started on our way. We Immediately

took the wrong turn, but the Bedouin stopped us and lead us back to the

ruins. We felt like he was our own personal guide. So in the end we

were in the park for 9 hours that day.

Walking through the park at the end of the day is nice. The sun

is setting and the air cooler. The shadows are cast long against the

sides of the tombs and Siq walls. We took our own water and food for the

day so our backpacks were much lighter. We consumed about 5 litres of

water each day. Be prepared to drink alot while you are there. you

don't notice losing the wate so you have to force yourself to drink every

half hour or so. The food inside the park is very expensive. You can

buy food in Wadi Musa and bring it with you. We took canned good and

pita. It worked well because we had a big meal before ,and after we were

in the park. Take a lot of film with you. If you need to buy film, but

it before going into the park, it will be less expensive.

 

The next day we were up and at the bus stop (actually a street

corner in Wadi Musa) by 6am to catch the bus to Aqaba. We wanted to be

at the border when it opened at 9am. We met the nice taxi driver who had

taken us in on Friday. He was going to Mann to visit family. He made

sure we got on the right bus. The bus did a small tour around the town,

stopping at some of the hotels. It wa was full by the time it stopped at

the last hotel. The trip cost 2 Dinar from Wadi Musa to Aqaba and 10

dinar for the taxi to the border. When we got to the border it was

clear. It took us twenty minutes and we were back at the bus to the

Eilat bus station.

The trip is well worth it. If you get a package tour to several

sites in Jordan try and spend as much time in the parks as possible. I

have been told that the tour guides that go with the package tour are

very good and are a great source of information about everything, not

just the sites.

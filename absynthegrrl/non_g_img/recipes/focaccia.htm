Focaccia

It's everywhere, taking over restaurant bread baskets, carving out its own niche at gourmet markets and transforming the upscale take-out sandwich. Focaccia, the rustic Italian flat bread that's crusty outside and chewy inside, has taken America by storm. The best news is that it's easy and fun to make at home. All you need is a willingness to dig your hands into the dough and the patience to let it rise. (If you have questions about baking bread, see the Q&A following the recipes.)

Fresh herbs, onions, olives, bacon and other aromatic ingredients add to the earthy character of focaccia, as the recipes here attest. The first one is a relatively simple focaccia made with onion, garlic, basil, thyme and rosemary mixed into the dough, which is then coated with olive oil and coarse salt. The three variations that are given use the same onion-herb dough as a base but feature different ingredients, which are kneaded in and applied on top. Each recipe makes a large sheet bread that can easily satisfy 8 to 10 people.

Ingredients:

3 tablespoons extra-virgin olive oil, plus more for brushing

1 cup (packed) minced yellow onion

2 medium garlic cloves, minced 1/4 cup finely chopped fresh basil

1 tablespoon minced fresh thyme

2 teaspoons minced fresh

rosemary Freshly ground pepper 1 1/2 cups lukewarm water

(105° to 115°) 1 cup lukewarm milk (105° to 115°)

1 scant tablespoon table salt

2 teaspoons plus a pinch of sugar

One 1/4- ounce envelope active dry yeast

About 6 1/2 cups bread flour

Cornmeal, for dusting

Coarse salt, for sprinkling

1/4 cup ice water

Directions:

1 . Heat the olive oil in a medium skillet over moderate heat. Stir in the onion and garlic, reduce the heat to low and cook until softened and fragrant, about 3 minutes. Add the herbs and a generous amount of pepper and cook for I minute. Transfer the mixture to a large bowl. Stir in 1 cup of the water, then the milk, table salt and 2 teaspoons sugar (Photo I on page 56).

2. In a small bowl, dissolve the yeast and the pinch of sugar in the remaining 1/2 cup lukewarm water until creamy and starting to bubble. Add to the onion mixture. Stir in the flour, 1 cup at a time (Photo 2), just until the dough becomes too stiff to stir. Turn out the dough onto a lightly floured work surface (Photo 3). With the help of a dough scraper, knead in only as much additional flour as necessary to keep the dough from sticking (Photo 4). Knead the dough until smooth, supple and elastic, about 5 minutes (Photos 5 through 8).

3. Generously grease a clean large bowl with olive oil. Transfer the dough to the bowl and turn to coat with oil. Cover the bowl with plastic wrap (Photo 9) and a kitchen towel. If desired, label the bowl lo remind you when to punch down and shape the dough (Photo 10). Set aside in a warm draft-free spot until doubled in bulk, about 2 hours.

4. Punch down the dough. Re-cover the bowl and let the dough rise again until doubled, about 1 hour.

5. Brush olive oil over the bottom and sides of a 17-by-13-by-l-inch black steel or heavy aluminum baking sheet. Sprinkle cornmeal on the pan, then tap out excess (Photo 11).

6. Punch down the dough and turn it out onto a cornmeal-dusted work surface (Photo 12). Turn the dough over and over to coat lightly with cornmeal and shape into a rough rectangle. Lift the dough and place in the prepared pan. Gently pull, push and pat the dough so it fills the baking sheet (Photo 13). Let the dough rise uncovered in a draft-free spot until billowy, 45 minutes to I hour.

7. Preheat the oven to 450° and position a rack in the bottom third. If using a baking stone or tiles, place on the rack and heat for 30 minutes.

8. Dip your fingers in flour and make many deep indentations in the dough. Brush lightly with olive oil and sprinkle with coarse salt.

9. Place the baking sheet in the oven, toss the ice water on the oven floor and bake the focaccia for about 30 minutes, until the edges are crisp and a deep golden color. Using a long metal spatula, slide the focaccia onto a large rack to cool. To serve, cut into pieces with a serrated knife.

Variants:

olive and onion focaccia

dough:

Basic Herb Focaccia (opposite page), plus 1/3 cup chopped pitted black oil-cured olives (about 24)

topping:

1/4 cup extra-virgin olive oil

1 large or 2 medium yellow onions, cut into thin wedges and separated into strips (3 cups)

2 large garlic cloves, thinly sliced

1 tablespoon finely chopped mixed fresh herbs

coarse salt

freshly ground pepper

12 oil-cured olives, halved and pitted

prep:

Make the dough: Follow the recipe for Basic Herb Focaccia through step 7, with this change: Stir in the olives with the herbs in Step 1.

Meanwhile, for the topping: Heat large, deep skillet over moderate heat. Add the oil and onions, reduce the heat to low and cook until the onions are slightly softened. Add the garlic and cook until softened but not brown. Add the mixed herbs, Season with coarse salt and pepper and cook for 1 minute. Set aside to cool. Spread the onion topping over the dough, leaving a 1-inch border. Dip your fingers in flour and make many indentations through the topping. arrange the olive halves decorative- ly on the dough and sprinkle coarse salt evenly all over the top (Photo 14). Bake according to Step 9.

bacon-cheese focaccia

dough:

Basic Herb Focaccia (opposite page) 1 pound 1/4-inch-thick lean bacon slices, cut into 1/4-inch dice 1 1/2 cups finely shredded Jarlsberg cheese (about 6 ounces) Olive oil and cornmeal, for the baking sheet

topping:

1 large egg white, lightly beaten with 1 teaspoon water

2 tablespoons poppy seeds or sesame seeds, or a combination

Melted unsalted butter or olive oil, for brushing

Coarse salt, for sprinkling

prep:

1. Make the dough: Follow the recipe for Basic Herb Focaccia through Step 3, While the dough rises, cook the bacon in a medium saucepan over moderately high heat, stirring, until crisp, about 10 minutes. Using a slotted spoon, transfer the bacon to paper towels to drain. Let cool.

2. Punch down the dough, turn out onto a work surface and knead in the bacon and cheese, distributing them evenly. Return the dough to the bowl, re-cover and let rise again until doubled, about I hour.

3. Brush olive oil over the bottom and sides of a 17-by-13-by-l-inch black steel or heavy aluminum baking sheet. Line the bottom with parchment paper. Brush the paper with more oil and sprinkle the pan with cornmeal, tapping out any excess.

4. For the topping: Proceed with Step 6 of the Basic Herb Focaccia recipe, but before you set aside the dough to rise, gently brush it with the egg white and sprinkle the seeds on top.

5. Preheat the oven as directed in Step 7. Gently brush the risen dough with enough melted butter or olive oil to coat lightly. Dip your fingers in flour and make many indentations in the dough. Sprinkle the coarse salt on top. Bake according to Step 9.

parmesan pine nut focaccia

dough:

Basic Herb Focaccia (opposite page) 1/2 cup pine nuts (about 3 ounces) 1 cup freshly grated Parmesan

topping:

1/4 cup olive oil, plus more for brushing

2 Cups finely chopped onion

4 large garlic cloves, minced

1/2 teaspoon dried oregano

Freshly ground pepper

Coarse salt, for sprinkling

1/2 cup freshly grated Parmesan

prep:

1. Make the dough: Follow the recipe . for Basic Herb Focaccia through Step 3. While the dough rises, preheat the oven to 350°. Toast the pine nuts on a baking sheet for about 7 minutes, until golden. Let cool.

2. Punch down the dough, turn it out onto a work surface and knead in the nuts and Parmesan, distributing them evenly. Return the dough to the bowl, re-cover and let rise again until doubled, about I hour.

3. Meanwhile, for the topping: In a medium skillet, heat 1/4 cup of the oil. Add the onion and garlic and cook over moderately high heat for about 5 minutes. Season with the oregano and pepper. Let cool.

4. Proceed with Steps 5 and 6 of the Basic Herb Focaccia recipe, but before you let the dough rise, gently brush it with enough olive oil to coat lightly. Spread the onion mixture on top, leaving a 1 -inch border.

5. Preheat the oven as directed in Step 7. Dip your fingers in flour and make many indentations in the dough. Sprinkle pepper and coarse salt on top. Proceed with Step 9, but bake the dough for 25 minutes; sprinkle the Parmesan on top and bake until golden, about 10 minutes longer.
